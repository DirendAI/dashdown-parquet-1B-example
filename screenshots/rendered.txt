Taxis vs ride-hail since 2020 #

Six years of monthly trips, one line per service — the yellow cab, the green cab, Uber and Lyft. Each line is that service's own volume, so the COVID-19 collapse in early 2020 and the long climb back read straight off the axis.

In January 2020, New York City’s ride-hail market was dominated by Uber with 54.6 % of the 26.7 million trips, while Lyft held 19.8 % and yellow cabs 24 %; by April, as COVID-19 hit, total trips plummeted to 4.5 million, with yellow cabs collapsing to just 238,000 trips (5.3 % share) while Uber surged to 68.6 % on 3.1 million rides. A slow recovery unfolded through the year, and by December the market had settled into a new normal: Uber at 65 % (8.5 million trips), Lyft at 23.2 % (3.0 million), and yellow cabs at 11.2 % (1.5 million), with green cabs a distant 0.6 %, showing how the pandemic reshaped the city’s ride landscape.

Money and market share tell the same story from two more angles — who carries the trips, and who collects the fares:

Since 2020, yellow cabs have seen their trip share shrink from about a quarter to roughly a tenth, while ride-hail apps—Uber and Lyft combined—have captured the majority, with Uber alone now accounting for roughly two-thirds of trips. Monthly rider spend has shifted accordingly, with Uber’s revenue consistently outpacing yellow cabs’ by a wide margin, reflecting its dominant market position as the current leader, carrying about 65% of trips.

Fares and tips are where the two worlds diverge most:

AI

Across the 2020 data, NYC yellow and green cabs show a consistent tipping culture with tip percentages hovering around 23–24 % for yellow cabs and 12–17 % for green cabs, while ride-hail services lag far behind, with Uber at roughly 2–3 % and Lyft at about 3–4 %, highlighting a striking gap that persists even though cab tips are recorded only on card payments and ride-hail tips are always in-app.

A note on the comparison: cab "base fare" is the meter fare; Uber/Lyft is the base passenger fare before fees. Cab tips are only recorded on card payments; Uber/Lyft tips are always in-app — that's the honest reason the gap looks so brutal.

How it's built — the raw TLC Parquet files are downloaded as-is (scripts/fetch_data.py), and every chart above is a plain SQL query in queries/ that reads those files directly with DuckDB's read_parquet() — no tables to define, no ETL. Dashdown's dashdown build executes the queries once and bakes the results into this static page; a GitHub Actions cron does that every night. The prose between the charts is written by an LLM at build time from each query's result — commentary that refreshes with the data.
